How I Started Investing on Easy Equities With Just R500 (And You Can Too)

By Amora · March 2, 2026 · 8 min read · Easy Equities, Investing 101
📱

I remember the exact moment I decided to stop being scared of investing. It was a Sunday, I was in my pyjamas eating cereal, and I saw a tweet from a girl who said she bought her first stock with R500 on Easy Equities. I thought: if she can do it, I can do it.

Three years later, that R500 has turned into a portfolio I'm genuinely proud of. And today I'm going to show you exactly how I did it — step by step, no finance degree required.

✨ Quick Note

This is not financial advice. I'm sharing my personal experience. Always do your own research and consider speaking to a certified financial planner before investing.

What Is Easy Equities?

Easy Equities is a South African investment platform that lets you buy shares, ETFs, and unit trusts starting from as little as R5. Yes, R5. You don't need to buy a whole share — you buy a fraction, which means even expensive stocks like Naspers are accessible to everyday people.

It's available as an app (iOS and Android) and a web platform. It's regulated by the FSCA, so your money is protected. And the fees? Way lower than traditional brokers.

Step 1: Download the App & Open Your Account

Go to the App Store or Google Play and search "Easy Equities." Download it, open it, and tap "Register." You'll need:

  • Your South African ID number
  • A selfie for FICA verification
  • Your bank account details
  • A few minutes (seriously, it's fast)

The account verification usually takes 24–48 hours. Once you're approved, you can fund your account.

Step 2: Choose Your Account Type

Easy Equities offers several account types. For beginners, I recommend starting with:

  • ZAR Account — Buy SA-listed shares and ETFs in rands. Perfect starting point.
  • TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account) — Invest up to R36,000/year completely tax-free. This is a MUST for beginners. Start here.
  • USD Account — Buy US stocks like Apple, Tesla, and S&P 500 ETFs. Great for diversification once you're comfortable.
"The best investment you can make is in a Tax-Free Savings Account. The government literally gives you free money by not taxing your returns."
📢
AdSense — In-Content (336×280)

Step 3: Fund Your Account

Tap the "Add Funds" button and do an EFT to the Easy Equities bank account. The reference number is your unique account number. Funds usually reflect within a few hours during banking hours.

I started with R500. You absolutely don't need more than that. The platform lets you buy fractional shares, so you're never locked out of a stock because you can't afford a full share.

Step 4: Buy Your First Investment

For your very first investment, I recommend keeping it simple: a broad market ETF. Here's why:

  • ETFs are diversified — you own a tiny piece of many companies
  • They have low fees (expense ratios of 0.1–0.5%)
  • They outperform most actively managed funds over the long term
  • No stock-picking stress

My first buy was the Satrix MSCI World ETF — it gives you exposure to 1,500+ companies across 23 developed countries. R500 into that ETF and I was officially a global investor. It felt incredible.

Step 5: Set Up a Monthly Debit Order

This is the secret sauce. Set up an automatic monthly investment — even R200/month. On Easy Equities, you can do this via their Bundle feature. You pick the ETF, set the amount, and it buys automatically on a set date every month.

This is called rand-cost averaging — you buy when prices are high and when they're low, so your average cost stays reasonable over time. You completely remove the stress of "is now a good time to invest?"

My Portfolio 3 Years Later

I started with R500 in early 2022. Today, my Easy Equities portfolio is worth significantly more — not because I'm a genius, but because I stayed consistent. I invested every single month, didn't panic-sell when markets dropped, and kept learning.

The biggest lesson? Starting is the hardest part. Once you buy your first share, it becomes real, exciting, and deeply motivating to keep going.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Easy Equities safe? Is my money protected? +
Yes. Easy Equities is regulated by the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA) in South Africa. Your investments are held in your name through Purple Group, the JSE-listed parent company. Your shares are registered in your name, not the company's, which means they're protected even if Easy Equities were to close.
What is the minimum amount to invest on Easy Equities? +
You can invest from as little as R5 on Easy Equities. The platform uses fractional investing, which means you buy a portion of a share — so even the most expensive stocks on the JSE and NYSE are accessible with small amounts.
What's the difference between a TFSA and a regular account? +
A Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) allows you to invest up to R36,000 per year (R500,000 lifetime) without paying any tax on dividends, interest, or capital gains. It's one of the best tools for long-term wealth building in SA. A regular account is taxed on profits. For most beginners, maximizing the TFSA first is the smart move.
Which ETF should a beginner buy first? +
For most South African beginners, I recommend starting with either the Satrix MSCI World ETF (global exposure) or the Satrix S&P 500 ETF (US exposure). Both have low fees and give you instant diversification across hundreds of companies. If you want SA-specific exposure, the Satrix 40 ETF tracks the top 40 JSE companies.
Can I withdraw my money at any time? +
Yes — you can sell your investments and withdraw at any time. However, investing should be a long-term game (5+ years). Selling when markets dip locks in losses. The longer you stay invested, the more time compound growth has to work in your favour.

The 5 Best ETFs to Buy in South Africa Right Now

By Amora· February 28, 2026· 6 min read
📊

If you're just starting out, the ETF world can feel overwhelming. There are hundreds of options, confusing ticker symbols, and everyone seems to have a different opinion. So let me cut through the noise and give you my top 5 ETF picks for South African investors in 2026.

Disclaimer: This is not financial advice. I'm sharing my personal research. Please consult a certified financial planner for personalised advice.

What Is an ETF and Why Should You Care?

An Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) is a basket of investments — shares, bonds, or commodities — that trades on a stock exchange just like a regular share. When you buy one ETF, you instantly own a tiny piece of every company in that basket. It's diversification made simple.

1. Satrix MSCI World ETF (STXWDM)

This is my personal favourite and forms the core of my TFSA portfolio. It tracks 1,500+ companies across 23 developed countries — think Apple, Microsoft, Samsung, Nestlé, and Toyota all in one fund. Expense ratio: 0.35%. Available on Easy Equities from R5.

📢
AdSense — In-Content (336×280)

2. Satrix S&P 500 ETF (STX500)

Tracks the 500 largest US companies. The S&P 500 has historically returned ~10% per year over the long term. If you believe in the US economy — and history suggests you should — this is a no-brainer. Expense ratio: 0.30%.

3. Satrix 40 ETF (STX40)

This tracks the top 40 companies on the JSE — Naspers, Standard Bank, Richemont, Anglo American, and more. It's your South African anchor. Great for rand-denominated exposure and slightly lower volatility. Expense ratio: 0.10% — one of the cheapest on the market.

4. CoreShares S&P 500 (CSSP500)

Another US market tracker, but this one compounds your dividends automatically (accumulating ETF). Slightly different holdings weighting to the Satrix S&P 500 — I hold both. Expense ratio: 0.20%.

5. Satrix Emerging Markets ETF (STXEMG)

For higher growth potential (and higher risk), the Emerging Markets ETF gives you exposure to companies in China, India, Brazil, South Korea, and more. It's more volatile, but over a 10+ year horizon, it's been a strong performer. Expense ratio: 0.40%.

"You don't need to pick winners. You just need to own the whole game."
📢

My Simple ETF Strategy

I split my monthly TFSA contribution like this: 50% MSCI World, 30% S&P 500, 20% Satrix 40. Simple. Boring. Effective. I haven't changed this allocation in two years and I'm comfortable watching it grow quietly in the background while I get on with my life.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are ETFs safer than individual stocks?+
Generally, yes. Because ETFs hold many stocks, the failure of one company has minimal impact on your overall investment. With individual stocks, one bad earnings report can wipe out 20% of your investment overnight. ETFs spread that risk automatically.
Can I buy ETFs through my TFSA on Easy Equities?+
Yes! Easy Equities offers a TFSA account type. All the ETFs I mentioned above are available in the TFSA. This is the most tax-efficient way to invest in ETFs as a South African resident.
How often should I buy more ETFs?+
I invest monthly on the same date every month. This is called rand-cost averaging and removes the stress of timing the market. Most experts agree: time in the market beats timing the market.

7 Ways I Make Money Online (Real Numbers, No BS)

By Amora· February 25, 2026· 10 min read
💻

I'm going to be fully transparent with you. I hate "make money online" articles that promise the world and deliver nothing. So this is my actual income report — what I do, how I do it, and roughly how much I make from each stream.

None of these happened overnight. I built each income stream over months (some over years). But if I can do it starting from zero, you can too.

1. This Blog (Display Advertising) — ~R3,500–R6,000/month

My blog earns through Google AdSense and Mediavine display advertising. Once your traffic hits around 10,000 monthly sessions, you can apply for better ad networks. The key is consistent, high-quality content in a niche advertisers love (finance is one of the best-paying niches).

2. Affiliate Marketing — ~R2,000–R4,500/month

I recommend products and platforms I actually use — Easy Equities, budgeting apps, financial courses — and earn a commission when readers sign up through my links. Honesty is everything here. I only promote what I genuinely believe in.

3. Digital Products (eBooks & Guides) — ~R1,800–R3,000/month

I created a beginner's guide to investing on Easy Equities and a budgeting spreadsheet. I sell both on Gumroad. Once created, digital products sell passively — I do zero extra work and money comes in while I sleep. This is genuinely my favourite income stream.

4. Freelance Writing — ~R4,000–R8,000/month

Finance and investment companies need content writers. I write blog posts, email newsletters, and social media content for fintech brands. I charge per word or per project. Finding clients: LinkedIn, direct pitching, and word of mouth.

5. Pinterest Traffic Monetisation — ~R800–R1,500/month

Pinterest drives significant traffic to my blog, which increases ad revenue. I spend about 2 hours a week creating pins. It's an underrated traffic source for women's finance content.

6. Online Tutoring (Finance & Budgeting) — ~R1,500–R2,500/month

I offer 1-on-1 "Money Clarity Sessions" via Zoom where I help people set up their budgets, understand their investments, and create a financial plan. I book these through Calendly and charge R500–R800 per session.

7. Stock Market Returns — Varies

My Easy Equities portfolio also generates passive income through dividends and capital appreciation. I don't count this as "income" per se, but it's wealth being built quietly every month. And when I eventually sell (decades from now), it'll be a significant sum.

"Multiple income streams aren't about being greedy — they're about being protected."

The Total & What It Took

On a good month, I bring in between R15,000–R25,000 from all online streams combined. But it took me 3 years of consistent effort to get here. I want you to know that not for discouragement — but for honesty. This is real work. But it's also genuinely flexible, scalable, and something you can build alongside a full-time job.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which online income stream should a beginner start with?+
For most beginners, I'd recommend starting with either freelance writing (fastest to earn money) or starting a blog (longest-term, but potentially highest ceiling). If you have a specific skill, offer it as a service first to generate income while building your long-term assets.
Do I need to pay tax on online income in South Africa?+
Yes. If you earn income from any source — including online income, freelancing, blog ads, or affiliate marketing — it must be declared to SARS. The tax threshold for the 2026/2026 year is R95,750 for those under 65. Always keep records of your income and consult a tax practitioner if unsure.
How long does it take to make money blogging?+
Most bloggers start seeing real income around 12–18 months in. SEO takes time. The blogs that succeed are the ones that consistently publish valuable content and build an audience before monetising. Treat it as a long-term asset, not a quick income stream.

Is Crypto Worth It in 2026? Everything a Beginner Girl Needs to Know

By Amora·March 14, 2026·7 min read
₿

Let's be honest. When someone first mentioned Bitcoin to me I thought it was fake internet money for nerds. I ignored it for years. Then I watched people turn R1,000 into R50,000 and I started paying attention.

But I also watched people lose everything chasing hype. So today I'm giving you the balanced, honest truth about crypto in 2026.

✨ Disclaimer

This is not financial advice. Cryptocurrency is highly volatile. Please do your own research before investing.

What Exactly Is Cryptocurrency?

Cryptocurrency is digital money that exists only online. Unlike rands or dollars, no government or bank controls it. It runs on technology called blockchain — a public record of every transaction that nobody can fake or delete. The most well-known are Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH), and stablecoins like USDC.

Why Do People Invest in Crypto?

Three main reasons: potential for massive growth, protection against inflation, and access to dollar-denominated assets without needing a foreign bank account. For South Africans watching the rand weaken, that last point is particularly powerful.

Advertisement
AdSense — In-Content 336×280

The Real Risks Nobody Talks About

Crypto is extremely volatile. Bitcoin has dropped 80% in value before — and recovered. But not everyone held through that drop. Many people panic sold at the bottom and lost everything. There are also scams everywhere. If anyone promises guaranteed crypto returns — run. It is always a scam.

How Much Should You Put In?

Most experts suggest no more than 5–10% of your portfolio in crypto. Build your foundation first: emergency fund, TFSA maxed with ETFs, then consider crypto with money you can genuinely afford to lose.

How to Buy Crypto Safely in South Africa

  • Luno — most popular in SA, very beginner friendly
  • VALR — more advanced, lower fees
  • Easy Crypto SA — buy and hold in your own wallet

My Bottom Line

Crypto is real. The opportunity is real. But so is the risk. Go in with your eyes open, start small, and never invest money you cannot afford to lose. You are already ahead of most people just by educating yourself first.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is crypto legal in South Africa?+
Yes. Crypto assets are legal in South Africa and regulated by the FSCA. Platforms like Luno and VALR are registered with the FSCA.
Do I pay tax on crypto profits?+
Yes. SARS treats crypto gains as taxable income or capital gains depending on your activity. Keep records of all your transactions.

What Is an Emergency Fund?

An emergency fund is 3 to 6 months of your living expenses saved in a separate, accessible account. It is not for holidays. It is not for sales. It is for genuine emergencies: job loss, medical bills, car repairs, unexpected rent increases.

Why Does It Matter for Investors?

Without an emergency fund, the next unexpected expense forces you to sell your investments. You will sell at the worst possible time — when you are stressed, when markets might be down, when you have no choice. An emergency fund is what lets you stay invested through market downturns.

Where to Keep Your Emergency Fund

A high-yield savings account or money market account. In South Africa look at: FNB Easy Save, Capitec savings account, or African Bank's savings accounts which often offer the best rates. Do not invest it in the stock market — it needs to be accessible within 24 hours.

  • Set up an automatic transfer the day after payday
  • Start with R500 per month — increase as you can

  • Treat it like a bill — non-negotiable

Leave it alone. Only touch it for genuine emergencies. When you do use it, rebuilding it becomes your next financial priority before any other goals.

"An emergency fund does not earn you money. It protects the money you are earning everywhere else."

In South Africa you are entitled to one free credit report per year from each credit bureau. The main bureaus are TransUnion, Experian, and Compuscan. You can also use apps like ClearScore or Nedbank Money app to check your score for free anytime.

What Affects Your Credit Score?

  • Payment history (35%) — paying on time is the biggest factor
  • Credit utilisation (30%) — how much of your credit limit you use
  • Length of credit history (15%) — older accounts help
  • Credit mix (10%) — variety of credit types
  • New credit inquiries (10%) — applying for too much credit hurts

How to Improve Your Score Fast

Pay every account on time — even the minimum. Keep your credit card balance below 30% of the limit. Do not apply for multiple credit products at once. Do not close your oldest credit account. Dispute any errors on your credit report immediately.

SARS Tax Season 2026: A Simple Guide for South African Investors

Do You Need to Submit a Tax Return?

You must submit if: you earned more than R500,000 from one employer, you had more than one income source, you earned rental income, you have investment income, you are self-employed, or SARS sends you a letter saying you must. When in doubt — submit. The penalty for not submitting is worse than any tax you might owe.

What Investors Need to Declare

  • Dividends — Easy Equities sends you a tax certificate (IT3b) every year
  • Capital gains — profit from selling shares or ETFs
  • Crypto gains — treated as income by SARS
  • Interest — from savings accounts above R23,800 per year
Advertisement

TFSA and Tax

Good news: your TFSA investments do not need to be declared on your tax return. That is literally the whole point. No dividends tax, no capital gains tax, no paperwork. One less thing to stress about.

How to Submit on SARS eFiling

Go to efiling.sars.gov.za. Register if you haven't already. Wait for your IRP5 from your employer (usually available by June). Add your investment income from your IT3b certificates. Submit your return. SARS will tell you if you owe money or get a refund.

Smart Tax Tips for Investors

Maximise your TFSA before investing outside it. Use your R40,000 annual capital gains exclusion — you only pay CGT on gains above this amount. Keep records of every investment transaction. Consider a tax practitioner if your situation is complex.

A Girl's Guide to Foreign Exchange (Forex): What It Is, How It Works, and How to Start Safely

By Amora·March 18, 2026·10 min read
💱

Every second girl on Instagram seems to be posting Forex profits these days. Lamborghinis, laptops on beaches, R50,000 weeks. It looks incredible. But nobody is showing you the losses, the blown accounts, or the months of studying before a single profitable trade. Today I am giving you the honest, complete picture — because you deserve the truth before you risk your money.

✨ Important Disclaimer

Forex trading is highly speculative and most retail traders lose money. This article is for educational purposes only and is not financial advice. Never trade with money you cannot afford to lose.

What Is Forex Trading?

Forex — short for foreign exchange — is the global market where currencies are bought and sold. When you exchange rands for dollars at the airport, you are participating in the forex market. But forex trading is about profiting from the changing values of currency pairs — like USD/ZAR, EUR/USD, or GBP/JPY.

The forex market is the largest financial market in the world, trading over $7 trillion per day. It runs 24 hours a day, 5 days a week. This accessibility is part of what makes it so attractive — and so dangerous.

How Does Forex Trading Actually Work?

You always trade currencies in pairs. If you believe the US dollar will strengthen against the rand, you buy USD/ZAR. If the dollar rises, you profit. If it falls, you lose. Simple in theory. Brutally complex in practice.

Most forex traders use leverage — meaning you control a large position with a small amount of money. A broker might offer 100:1 leverage, meaning R1,000 controls R100,000 worth of currency. This amplifies profits but also amplifies losses dramatically. A 1% move against you on a leveraged position can wipe out your entire account.

The Honest Truth About Forex Trading Statistics

Studies consistently show that 70% to 80% of retail forex traders lose money. This is not a conspiracy — it is a mathematical reality of leveraged trading combined with market complexity, emotional decision-making, and the fact that you are competing against professional traders, banks, and algorithms with billions in resources.

The people selling you R999 Forex courses on Instagram? Most of them make their money selling courses — not trading. Always ask: if this strategy is so profitable, why are they selling it instead of just trading it?

AdSense — In-Content 336×280

Is Forex Trading Right for You?

Forex might be worth exploring if you are genuinely passionate about financial markets, willing to study for 6 to 12 months before risking real money, emotionally disciplined enough to take losses without panic, and able to afford to lose your starting capital completely.

Forex is probably not right for you if you are looking for quick income, under financial pressure, or expecting consistent monthly returns as a beginner.

How to Start Forex Safely — If You Choose To

Step 1 — Educate yourself first. Spend at least 3 months learning before touching real money. Study technical analysis, fundamental analysis, risk management, and trading psychology. Baby Pips (babypips.com) offers a completely free, comprehensive forex course. Start there.

Step 2 — Open a demo account. Every reputable broker offers a free demo account with virtual money. Trade on demo for at least 3 months. If you cannot be consistently profitable on demo, you will not be profitable with real money. Most people skip this step and lose real money immediately.

Step 3 — Choose a regulated broker. In South Africa, ensure your broker is regulated by the FSCA. Reputable brokers include Exness, HFM, and IG Markets. Avoid any broker not regulated by a recognised authority — there are many scams in this space.

Step 4 — Start micro. When you move to real money, start with the absolute minimum. R500 to R1,000. Trade micro lots. Your goal is not to make money immediately — it is to experience real trading psychology with minimal risk.

Step 5 — Risk management above everything. Never risk more than 1% to 2% of your account on a single trade. This is the rule that separates traders who survive long term from those who blow their accounts in a week.

Forex vs ETF Investing — Which Is Better for Beginners?

For most women starting their financial journey, ETF investing through Easy Equities is significantly better than forex trading. Here is why: ETFs have a proven long-term track record, require no daily monitoring, have no leverage risk, and the majority of investors profit over time. Forex is the opposite on almost every metric for beginners.

My personal approach: I use Easy Equities for long-term wealth building and I paper trade forex on the side to learn. I do not trade forex with significant real money and I am honest about why — I have not put in enough screen time yet to do it responsibly.

The Forex Scams You Need to Know About

South Africa has a serious problem with forex scams. Watch out for anyone promising guaranteed monthly returns, signal sellers charging monthly fees for "winning trades," account managers asking to trade on your behalf, and WhatsApp groups promising to double your money. If it sounds too good to be true — it always is.

"Forex is not a get-rich-quick scheme. It is a skill that takes years to develop. Respect it and it might reward you. Rush it and it will humble you."

My Bottom Line for Girls Wanting to Get Into Forex

Do not start with forex if you have no emergency fund, no investments, and no financial foundation. Build that first. Then if forex genuinely interests you — study it properly, demo trade for months, and approach it as a skill you are developing over years, not a lottery ticket.

The girls making real money in forex are not the ones who started last month. They are the ones who studied for a year, blew a small demo account, learned from it, and slowly built discipline over time. That can be you — if you are willing to do it properly.

What You Actually Need to Start a Money-Making Blog

You need less than you think. A domain name (about R150 per year), hosting (free on Netlify to start), and content. That is it. No expensive equipment, no professional photos, no design degree. The blogs making the most money are not always the prettiest — they are the most helpful.

Choosing Your Niche — The Most Important Decision

The biggest blogging mistake is trying to write about everything. Pick one niche and own it. The best niches for monetisation in 2026 are personal finance, investing, health and wellness, and career development. Finance is my favourite because the advertising rates are exceptional — finance advertisers pay 3 to 5 times more per click than lifestyle or fashion advertisers.

Within finance, get even more specific. Not just "personal finance" but "personal finance for South African women in their 20s." The more specific your niche, the more loyal your audience and the more relevant your advertising.

Content Strategy: What to Write About

Write content that answers questions people are actively searching for. Use Google to research — type your topic and look at the "People also ask" section. Those are real questions real people need answered. Every question is a potential blog post.

For a finance blog I recommend: beginner guides (How to open an Easy Equities account), comparison posts (ETF A vs ETF B), personal stories (How I paid off my debt), and listicles (5 things to do with your first salary). These formats consistently perform well in search.

Getting Traffic: How People Find Your Blog

SEO (Search Engine Optimisation) is the most powerful long-term traffic source. When someone Googles "how to invest in South Africa" and your post appears — that is free traffic forever. SEO takes 6 to 12 months to build but once it works it works without you doing anything.

Pinterest is my secret weapon for finance blogs. Pinterest users are actively looking for financial advice and they click through to blogs at much higher rates than other social platforms. Create beautiful pins for every blog post and pin consistently.

Instagram and TikTok build your personal brand and drive readers to your blog. Short finance tips that link back to longer blog posts are a winning strategy. You do not need millions of followers — you need the right followers.

How Much Can You Realistically Earn?

Here is an honest income timeline for a finance blogger who publishes consistently and applies basic SEO:

  • Months 1 to 6: R0 to R500 per month — building foundation, minimal traffic

These numbers are achievable. They are not guaranteed. They require consistent publishing, learning SEO, building an email list, and treating your blog like a business. The bloggers who quit in year one never see what year three looks like.

The One Thing That Separates Successful Bloggers From Everyone Else

Consistency. Not talent. Not connections. Not a big budget. Consistency. Publishing one quality post per week for two years will outperform someone who publishes twenty posts in one month and then disappears. The algorithm rewards consistency. Your audience rewards consistency. Your income rewards consistency.

How to Monetize a Website in 2026: Every Method That Actually Works

By Amora·March 31, 2026·12 min read
💻

I built my first website with zero intention of making money from it. I just wanted a space to write about finance in a way that felt real and relatable. Then one day I received an AdSense approval email and everything changed. Today my website earns money every single day — while I sleep, while I study, while I do absolutely nothing. Here is every monetisation method I know, ranked from easiest to most lucrative.

✨ Real Talk First

Website monetisation takes time. Most sites take 6 to 18 months before earning meaningful income. The ones that succeed treat their site like a business from day one. This guide is for those people.

Method 1 — Google AdSense (Display Advertising)

AdSense is the most accessible monetisation method for new websites. Google places ads on your site automatically and pays you every time a visitor sees or clicks them. You earn through two models: CPM (cost per 1,000 impressions — you earn just for showing ads) and CPC (cost per click — you earn when someone clicks).

Finance websites earn some of the highest AdSense rates of any niche — between R8 and R25 per click depending on the advertiser. A finance site with 10,000 monthly visitors can realistically earn R3,000 to R8,000 per month from AdSense alone.

How to get approved: Your site needs original content, a Privacy Policy page, an About page, a Contact page, and must not violate Google's content policies. Apply at adsense.google.com once you have at least 15 to 20 quality posts.

Method 2 — Affiliate Marketing

Affiliate marketing is recommending products and earning a commission when your readers buy or sign up through your unique link. For a finance website, this is incredibly powerful because financial products pay some of the highest commissions available anywhere online.

Examples of finance affiliate programmes in South Africa:

  • Easy Equities — earn commission for every new investor you refer
  • Luno — earn when referred users trade crypto
  • Discovery Bank — referral bonuses for new account sign ups
  • Takealot — 4% to 6% commission on any product purchased
  • Amazon Associates — earn on any Amazon purchase from your link

The beauty of affiliate marketing is that one well-placed link in a popular post can earn you money for years. I have affiliate links in posts I wrote over a year ago that still earn commissions every single month without me doing anything.

Method 3 — Digital Products

Digital products are the holy grail of website monetisation. You create something once and sell it an unlimited number of times with zero additional effort. Every sale is almost pure profit because there are no manufacturing or shipping costs.

For a finance website, digital products that sell well include:

  • Budget spreadsheet templates (R49 to R199)
  • Investment tracking spreadsheets (R99 to R299)
  • Financial planning guides and eBooks (R99 to R499)
  • Net worth tracker templates (R49 to R149)
  • Debt payoff calculators (R49 to R99)

Platforms to sell digital products: Gumroad (free to start), Payhip (free plan available), or directly through your own site using PayFast for South African payments.

Method 4 — Sponsored Content

As your website grows, brands will pay you to write about their products. A sponsored post on a finance website typically earns between R1,500 and R15,000 depending on your traffic and audience size. Finance brands, banks, fintech apps, and investment platforms are particularly willing to pay for access to engaged financial audiences.

You do not need millions of visitors to attract sponsors. Brands are increasingly interested in micro-influencers — websites with smaller but highly engaged, niche audiences. A finance site with 5,000 loyal monthly readers is more valuable to a financial brand than a lifestyle site with 50,000 casual readers.

Method 5 — Email Newsletter Monetisation

Your email list is the most valuable asset your website can build. Unlike social media followers, your email subscribers are yours — no algorithm can take them away. A finance newsletter with 5,000 engaged subscribers can be monetised through sponsored newsletter placements (R500 to R3,000 per mention), affiliate links within emails, promoting your own digital products, and paid subscriber tiers for premium content.

Start building your email list from day one. Offer a free resource — a budget template, an investing checklist, a savings calculator — in exchange for an email address. Even 100 engaged subscribers is worth more than 10,000 social media followers who never click anything.

Method 6 — Online Courses and Workshops

Once you have established yourself as a trusted voice in your niche, selling knowledge becomes very lucrative. A 2-hour online course on investing basics for South African women could sell for R299 to R999. If 100 people buy it that is R30,000 to R100,000 from one course you created once.

Platforms to host your course: Teachable, Gumroad, or simply a private Facebook group with gated content. Start with a workshop before building a full course — it is faster to create and helps you understand exactly what your audience needs.

Method 7 — Freelance Services

Your website is your portfolio. Use it to attract freelance clients for services related to your niche. As a finance blogger you could offer: personal financial coaching sessions (R400 to R800 per hour), budget reviews and financial planning consultations, freelance writing for other finance brands, and social media management for financial companies.

Many successful bloggers earn more from services than from any passive income stream — especially in the early years before traffic builds up. Your website builds credibility that makes selling services dramatically easier.

The Monetisation Roadmap — What to Focus on When

Month 1 to 6: Focus entirely on content and SEO. Apply for AdSense. Build your email list. Do not obsess over income yet — you are building the foundation.

Month 6 to 12: Add affiliate links to existing posts. Create your first digital product. Start offering a service. Income should start trickling in.

Year 2: Pitch sponsors. Launch a course or workshop. Grow your email list aggressively. This is where income starts compounding.

Year 3+: Multiple income streams all working simultaneously. This is where the real money is. Most people quit before getting here.

"A website is not a lottery ticket. It is a vending machine. Stock it with valuable content, put it in front of the right people, and it will dispense money indefinitely."

How Much Can You Realistically Earn?

Here is a realistic income breakdown for a finance website at different traffic levels:

  • 1,000 visitors/month: R500 to R1,500 (mainly AdSense + small affiliate)
  • 5,000 visitors/month: R2,000 to R6,000 (AdSense + affiliates + digital products)
  • 20,000 visitors/month: R8,000 to R25,000 (all streams active)
  • 50,000 visitors/month: R25,000 to R80,000+ (sponsorships unlocked)

These numbers are achievable. They are not overnight. They are the result of consistent publishing, smart SEO, and treating your website like the business it is.

The One Thing Most People Get Wrong

They try to monetise too early. They put up ads before they have content. They add affiliate links before they have traffic. They pitch sponsors before they have an audience. The result is a site that earns nothing and eventually gets abandoned.

Build first. Monetise second. The websites earning serious money are built on a foundation of genuine value — content so good that people bookmark it, share it, and come back for more. Get that right first and the money follows naturally.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much traffic do I need before monetising my website?+
You can apply for AdSense with any amount of traffic — Google does not have a minimum traffic requirement. However, you need enough quality content for Google to evaluate your site. For affiliate marketing you can start immediately. For sponsorships, most brands want at least 5,000 monthly visitors.
How does Google AdSense pay you in South Africa?+
Google AdSense pays via EFT directly to your South African bank account once you reach the R1,300 payment threshold (approximately $70 USD). Payments are made monthly. You will need to verify your identity and banking details with Google before your first payment.
Do I need to register a business to earn money from my website?+
No, you can earn as a sole proprietor using your personal ID number. However once you earn consistently above R80,000 per year you should consider registering a company and VAT registration may become required above R1 million annual turnover.
Which monetisation method should I start with?+
Start with Google AdSense — it is passive, requires no audience relationship, and works from day one of approval. Add affiliate links in month 2 or 3. Create your first digital product in month 4 to 6. Build in that order and you will have three income streams working within your first year.
Can a static HTML website be monetised with AdSense?+
Absolutely yes. Google AdSense works on any website regardless of how it is built — static HTML, WordPress, Wix, or any other platform. What matters is your content quality and compliance with AdSense policies, not your technical stack.
