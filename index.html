How I Started Investing on Easy Equities With Just R500 (And You Can Too)

By Amora · March 2, 2026 · 8 min read · Easy Equities, Investing 101
📱

I remember the exact moment I decided to stop being scared of investing. It was a Sunday, I was in my pyjamas eating cereal, and I saw a tweet from a girl who said she bought her first stock with R500 on Easy Equities. I thought: if she can do it, I can do it.

Three years later, that R500 has turned into a portfolio I'm genuinely proud of. And today I'm going to show you exactly how I did it — step by step, no finance degree required.

✨ Quick Note

This is not financial advice. I'm sharing my personal experience. Always do your own research and consider speaking to a certified financial planner before investing.

What Is Easy Equities?

Easy Equities is a South African investment platform that lets you buy shares, ETFs, and unit trusts starting from as little as R5. Yes, R5. You don't need to buy a whole share — you buy a fraction, which means even expensive stocks like Naspers are accessible to everyday people.

It's available as an app (iOS and Android) and a web platform. It's regulated by the FSCA, so your money is protected. And the fees? Way lower than traditional brokers.

Step 1: Download the App & Open Your Account

Go to the App Store or Google Play and search "Easy Equities." Download it, open it, and tap "Register." You'll need:

  • Your South African ID number
  • A selfie for FICA verification
  • Your bank account details
  • A few minutes (seriously, it's fast)

The account verification usually takes 24–48 hours. Once you're approved, you can fund your account.

Step 2: Choose Your Account Type

Easy Equities offers several account types. For beginners, I recommend starting with:

  • ZAR Account — Buy SA-listed shares and ETFs in rands. Perfect starting point.
  • TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account) — Invest up to R36,000/year completely tax-free. This is a MUST for beginners. Start here.
  • USD Account — Buy US stocks like Apple, Tesla, and S&P 500 ETFs. Great for diversification once you're comfortable.
"The best investment you can make is in a Tax-Free Savings Account. The government literally gives you free money by not taxing your returns."

Step 3: Fund Your Account

Tap the "Add Funds" button and do an EFT to the Easy Equities bank account. The reference number is your unique account number. Funds usually reflect within a few hours during banking hours.

I started with R500. You absolutely don't need more than that. The platform lets you buy fractional shares, so you're never locked out of a stock because you can't afford a full share.

Step 4: Buy Your First Investment

For your very first investment, I recommend keeping it simple: a broad market ETF. Here's why:

  • ETFs are diversified — you own a tiny piece of many companies
  • They have low fees (expense ratios of 0.1–0.5%)
  • They outperform most actively managed funds over the long term
  • No stock-picking stress

My first buy was the Satrix MSCI World ETF — it gives you exposure to 1,500+ companies across 23 developed countries. R500 into that ETF and I was officially a global investor. It felt incredible.

Step 5: Set Up a Monthly Debit Order

This is the secret sauce. Set up an automatic monthly investment — even R200/month. On Easy Equities, you can do this via their Bundle feature. You pick the ETF, set the amount, and it buys automatically on a set date every month.

This is called rand-cost averaging — you buy when prices are high and when they're low, so your average cost stays reasonable over time. You completely remove the stress of "is now a good time to invest?"

My Portfolio 3 Years Later

I started with R500 in early 2022. Today, my Easy Equities portfolio is worth significantly more — not because I'm a genius, but because I stayed consistent. I invested every single month, didn't panic-sell when markets dropped, and kept learning.

The biggest lesson? Starting is the hardest part. Once you buy your first share, it becomes real, exciting, and deeply motivating to keep going.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Easy Equities safe? Is my money protected? +
Yes. Easy Equities is regulated by the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA) in South Africa. Your investments are held in your name through Purple Group, the JSE-listed parent company. Your shares are registered in your name, not the company's, which means they're protected even if Easy Equities were to close.
What is the minimum amount to invest on Easy Equities? +
You can invest from as little as R5 on Easy Equities. The platform uses fractional investing, which means you buy a portion of a share — so even the most expensive stocks on the JSE and NYSE are accessible with small amounts.
What's the difference between a TFSA and a regular account? +
A Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) allows you to invest up to R36,000 per year (R500,000 lifetime) without paying any tax on dividends, interest, or capital gains. It's one of the best tools for long-term wealth building in SA. A regular account is taxed on profits. For most beginners, maximizing the TFSA first is the smart move.
Which ETF should a beginner buy first? +
For most South African beginners, I recommend starting with either the Satrix MSCI World ETF (global exposure) or the Satrix S&P 500 ETF (US exposure). Both have low fees and give you instant diversification across hundreds of companies. If you want SA-specific exposure, the Satrix 40 ETF tracks the top 40 JSE companies.
Can I withdraw my money at any time? +
Yes — you can sell your investments and withdraw at any time. However, investing should be a long-term game (5+ years). Selling when markets dip locks in losses. The longer you stay invested, the more time compound growth has to work in your favour.

The 5 Best ETFs to Buy in South Africa Right Now

By Amora· February 28, 2026· 6 min read
📊

If you're just starting out, the ETF world can feel overwhelming. There are hundreds of options, confusing ticker symbols, and everyone seems to have a different opinion. So let me cut through the noise and give you my top 5 ETF picks for South African investors in 2026.

Disclaimer: This is not financial advice. I'm sharing my personal research. Please consult a certified financial planner for personalised advice.

What Is an ETF and Why Should You Care?

An Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF) is a basket of investments — shares, bonds, or commodities — that trades on a stock exchange just like a regular share. When you buy one ETF, you instantly own a tiny piece of every company in that basket. It's diversification made simple.

1. Satrix MSCI World ETF (STXWDM)

This is my personal favourite and forms the core of my TFSA portfolio. It tracks 1,500+ companies across 23 developed countries — think Apple, Microsoft, Samsung, Nestlé, and Toyota all in one fund. Expense ratio: 0.35%. Available on Easy Equities from R5.

📢

2. Satrix S&P 500 ETF (STX500)

Tracks the 500 largest US companies. The S&P 500 has historically returned ~10% per year over the long term. If you believe in the US economy — and history suggests you should — this is a no-brainer. Expense ratio: 0.30%.

3. Satrix 40 ETF (STX40)

This tracks the top 40 companies on the JSE — Naspers, Standard Bank, Richemont, Anglo American, and more. It's your South African anchor. Great for rand-denominated exposure and slightly lower volatility. Expense ratio: 0.10% — one of the cheapest on the market.

4. CoreShares S&P 500 (CSSP500)

Another US market tracker, but this one compounds your dividends automatically (accumulating ETF). Slightly different holdings weighting to the Satrix S&P 500 — I hold both. Expense ratio: 0.20%.

5. Satrix Emerging Markets ETF (STXEMG)

For higher growth potential (and higher risk), the Emerging Markets ETF gives you exposure to companies in China, India, Brazil, South Korea, and more. It's more volatile, but over a 10+ year horizon, it's been a strong performer. Expense ratio: 0.40%.

"You don't need to pick winners. You just need to own the whole game."

My Simple ETF Strategy

I split my monthly TFSA contribution like this: 50% MSCI World, 30% S&P 500, 20% Satrix 40. Simple. Boring. Effective. I haven't changed this allocation in two years and I'm comfortable watching it grow quietly in the background while I get on with my life.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are ETFs safer than individual stocks?+
Generally, yes. Because ETFs hold many stocks, the failure of one company has minimal impact on your overall investment. With individual stocks, one bad earnings report can wipe out 20% of your investment overnight. ETFs spread that risk automatically.
Can I buy ETFs through my TFSA on Easy Equities?+
Yes! Easy Equities offers a TFSA account type. All the ETFs I mentioned above are available in the TFSA. This is the most tax-efficient way to invest in ETFs as a South African resident.
How often should I buy more ETFs?+
I invest monthly on the same date every month. This is called rand-cost averaging and removes the stress of timing the market. Most experts agree: time in the market beats timing the market.

7 Ways I Make Money Online (Real Numbers, No BS)

By Amora· February 25, 2026· 10 min read
💻

I'm going to be fully transparent with you. I hate "make money online" articles that promise the world and deliver nothing. So this is my actual income report — what I do, how I do it, and roughly how much you could make from each stream.

None of these happened overnight. I built each income stream over months (some over years). But if I can do it starting from zero, you can too.

1. This Blog (Display Advertising) — ~R3,500–R6,000/month

My blog earns through Google AdSense and Mediavine display advertising. Once your traffic hits around 10,000 monthly sessions, you can apply for better ad networks. The key is consistent, high-quality content in a niche advertisers love (finance is one of the best-paying niches).

2. Affiliate Marketing — ~R2,000–R4,500/month

I recommend products and platforms I actually use — Easy Equities, budgeting apps, financial courses — and earn a commission when readers sign up through my links. Honesty is everything here. I only promote what I genuinely believe in.

4. Freelance Writing — ~R4,000–R8,000/month

Finance and investment companies need content writers. writing blog posts, email newsletters, and social media content for fintech brands. I charge per word or per project. Finding clients: LinkedIn, direct pitching, and word of mouth.

5. Pinterest Traffic Monetisation — ~R800–R1,500/month

people who have built this up bring in between R15,000–R25,000 from all online streams combined. But it took me 3 years of consistent effort to get here. Frequently Asked Questions

Which online income stream should a beginner start with?+
For most beginners, I'd recommend starting with either freelance writing (fastest to earn money) or starting a blog (longest-term, but potentially highest ceiling). If you have a specific skill, offer it as a service first to generate income while building your long-term assets.
Do I need to pay tax on online income in South Africa?+
Yes. If you earn income from any source — including online income, freelancing, blog ads, or affiliate marketing — it must be declared to SARS. The tax threshold for the 2025/2026 year is R95,750 for those under 65. Always keep records of your income and consult a tax practitioner if unsure.
How long does it take to make money blogging?+
Most bloggers start seeing real income around 12–18 months in. SEO takes time. The blogs that succeed are the ones that consistently publish valuable content and build an audience before monetising. Treat it as a long-term asset, not a quick income stream.

Is Crypto Worth It in 2026? Everything a Beginner Girl Needs to Know

By Amora·March 14, 2026·7 min read
₿

Let's be honest. When someone first mentioned Bitcoin to me I thought it was fake internet money for nerds. I ignored it for years. Then I watched people turn R1,000 into R50,000 and I started paying attention.

But I also watched people lose everything chasing hype. So today I'm giving you the balanced, honest truth about crypto in 2026.

✨ Disclaimer

This is not financial advice. Cryptocurrency is highly volatile. Please do your own research before investing.

What Exactly Is Cryptocurrency?

Cryptocurrency is digital money that exists only online. Unlike rands or dollars, no government or bank controls it. It runs on technology called blockchain — a public record of every transaction that nobody can fake or delete. The most well-known are Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH), and stablecoins like USDC.

Why Do People Invest in Crypto?

Three main reasons: potential for massive growth, protection against inflation, and access to dollar-denominated assets without needing a foreign bank account. For South Africans watching the rand weaken, that last point is particularly powerful.

The Real Risks Nobody Talks About

Crypto is extremely volatile. Bitcoin has dropped 80% in value before — and recovered. But not everyone held through that drop. Many people panic sold at the bottom and lost everything. There are also scams everywhere. If anyone promises potential crypto returns — run. It is always a scam.

How Much Should You Put In?

Most experts suggest no more than 5–10% of your portfolio in crypto. Build your foundation first: emergency fund, TFSA maxed with ETFs, then consider crypto with money you can genuinely afford to lose.

How to Buy Crypto Safely in South Africa

  • Luno — most popular in SA, very beginner friendly
  • VALR — more advanced, lower fees
  • Easy Crypto SA — buy and hold in your own wallet

My Bottom Line

Crypto is real. The opportunity is real. But so is the risk. Go in with your eyes open, start small, and never invest money you cannot afford to lose. You are already ahead of most people just by educating yourself first.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is crypto legal in South Africa?+
Yes. Crypto assets are legal in South Africa and regulated by the FSCA. Platforms like Luno and VALR are registered with the FSCA.
Do I pay tax on crypto profits?+
Yes. SARS treats crypto gains as taxable income or capital gains depending on your activity. Keep records of all your transactions.
What is the minimum I can invest in crypto?+
On Luno you can buy Bitcoin from as little as R50. You don't need to buy a whole Bitcoin — you buy a fraction.

The "Renting is Throwing Money Away" Myth

This is probably the most repeated piece of financial advice in South African households and it is worth examining carefully. When you rent, you are paying for something valuable: a place to live, flexibility, and freedom from maintenance costs, bond registration fees, transfer duties, and the risk of a property losing value.

When you buy, your monthly payment includes interest to the bank (which is also "gone"), insurance, rates and taxes, levies if in a complex, and maintenance costs. In the early years of a home loan the vast majority of your payment goes to interest — not building equity. So the idea that buying is always building wealth while renting is always wasting money is simply not accurate.

The Real Cost of Buying in South Africa

Consider a property worth R1,000,000. Before you even move in you would potentially need: a deposit of R100,000 to R200,000 (10% to 20%), transfer duty of approximately R15,000 (on a R1M property), bond registration costs of approximately R25,000, and moving costs. That is potentially R140,000 to R240,000 in upfront costs before paying a single month's bond.

Your monthly bond payment on R900,000 borrowed at prime (currently around 11.25%) over 20 years would be approximately R9,200 per month. Add rates, levies, insurance and maintenance and your true monthly cost could easily reach R11,000 to R13,000 per month — potentially more than renting a comparable property.

The Real Benefit of Buying

The genuine long-term benefit of buying is that after 20 years you own the property outright with no monthly payment. Property has also historically kept pace with inflation in South Africa, meaning it could preserve your wealth even if it does not dramatically grow it. And there is genuine lifestyle value in owning — stability, the ability to renovate, not being subject to a landlord's decisions.

What Renting Could Offer Instead

If you rent and invest the difference — the deposit and monthly savings compared to buying — the outcome could be very different. R200,000 invested in a diversified ETF portfolio at a historical average of 10% per year over 20 years could grow to approximately R1.3 million. Monthly savings of R2,000 invested over 20 years at 10% could grow to approximately R1.5 million. That is potentially R2.8 million built through renting and investing versus owning a property that may be worth R2 to R3 million by then.

The numbers can be surprisingly similar — which is exactly why this decision deserves serious thought rather than defaulting to "buying is always better."

Who Might Benefit More From Buying

Buying could make more sense if you plan to stay in one place for at least 7 to 10 years, have a stable income and job security, can afford the deposit and upfront costs without depleting your emergency fund, and are buying in an area with solid long-term demand. The longer you stay in a property the better the buying case becomes because the upfront costs get spread over more years.

Who Might Benefit More From Renting

Renting could make more sense if you are in your 20s with an uncertain career path, plan to move cities or countries in the next few years, do not have a stable emergency fund yet, or are in a high-interest-rate environment like 2026 where bond costs are elevated. Renting and investing the difference could genuinely build more wealth than buying in certain circumstances.

"The best financial decision is the one that fits your actual life — not the one that sounds most impressive at a braai."

What is transfer duty in South Africa?+
Transfer duty is a tax paid to SARS when you purchase property. Properties up to R1,100,000 are exempt from transfer duty. Above that threshold a sliding scale applies. It is important to factor this into your total purchase cost calculation.
How much deposit do I need to buy a house in South Africa?+
Banks typically prefer a 10% to 20% deposit. Some banks may grant 100% bonds but these come with higher interest rates and stricter qualifying criteria. A larger deposit reduces your monthly repayment and the total interest paid over the life of the bond.
Does buying always beat renting long term?+
Not necessarily. It depends on the property price, interest rates, how long you stay, and what you do with money you save by renting. In some scenarios renting and investing the difference could produce similar or better financial outcomes. Run the actual numbers for your specific situation before deciding.

Dividend Investing for Beginners: How You Could Potentially Earn While You Sleep

By Amora·February 5, 2026·8 min read
💵

What if your investments paid you money regularly — not just when you eventually sell them, but on an ongoing basis while you hold them? That is the core idea behind dividend investing and it is one of the most exciting concepts I have come across in my personal finance research.

I want to break it down in plain language because when I first heard the phrase "dividend investing" it sounded like something only wealthy retired people did. It is not. It is something any beginner investor could potentially start with even a small amount through Easy Equities.

✨ Disclaimer

This is educational content based on my research. It is not financial advice. Past dividend performance does not guarantee future payments. Please do your own research.

What Is a Dividend?

When a company makes a profit it has a choice: reinvest that money back into the business or share some of it with shareholders. When a company chooses to share profits with shareholders, those payments are called dividends. As a shareholder — even a tiny one — you could receive a portion of those profits proportional to how many shares you own.

For example, if you own shares in a company that pays an annual dividend of R2 per share and you own 100 shares, you could receive R200 in dividends that year. While you held those shares and did nothing, money potentially arrived in your investment account. That is what people mean when they say "earning while you sleep."

Which South African Shares Pay Dividends?

Many of the largest JSE-listed companies pay regular dividends. Companies in sectors like banking, retail, property (REITs), and resources have historically been consistent dividend payers. Examples of well-known SA dividend-paying companies include the major banks, Shoprite, Mr Price, and various property funds. However dividend policies can change — companies can cut or suspend dividends especially during difficult economic periods, which is an important risk to understand.

Dividend ETFs — A Simpler Approach for Beginners

Rather than picking individual dividend-paying shares, a dividend ETF bundles together many dividend-paying companies into one product. This could provide diversification — if one company cuts its dividend the others may compensate. On Easy Equities you can access dividend-focused ETFs that could provide exposure to many dividend-paying companies across South Africa and globally.

Dividend Tax in South Africa

This is important to understand. Dividends received from South African companies are subject to dividends withholding tax of 20%. This means if a company declares a R1 dividend per share you would receive R0.80 after tax. However if your dividends are received inside a TFSA they are completely tax free — which is one of the strongest arguments for using your TFSA for dividend investing.

Here is an illustrative example — not a guarantee or projection. If you invested R500 per month into a diversified basket of dividend-paying shares and ETFs with an average dividend yield of 4% per year, reinvesting all dividends, over 10 years at an assumed total return of 8 to 10% per year, your portfolio could potentially reach a size where annual dividends alone might cover a meaningful portion of monthly expenses. The key word throughout is "could" — markets fluctuate, dividends are not guaranteed, and actual returns will vary significantly.

The reason dividend investing excites so many people is the psychological satisfaction of receiving regular payments. It makes investing feel tangible and rewarding in a way that pure growth investing sometimes does not. Whether that emotional benefit justifies a dividend-focused strategy versus a total return approach is a discussion worth having with a financial advisor.

"A share that pays you to hold it changes the way you think about investing. It stops feeling like gambling and starts feeling like ownership."

Frequently Asked Questions

When are dividends paid in South Africa?+
Most JSE-listed companies pay dividends twice a year — an interim dividend mid-year and a final dividend at year end. Some companies pay quarterly. The company announces a dividend, sets a "last day to trade" date, and pays on a specified payment date.
Can I live off dividends in South Africa?+
In theory yes — this is the goal of many dividend investors. To generate R10,000 per month in dividends at a 5% annual yield you would need a portfolio of approximately R2.4 million. This is achievable over time with consistent investing but requires significant capital and time to build.
Should I reinvest dividends or take the cash?+
For most young investors reinvesting dividends — also called DRIP (Dividend Reinvestment Plan) — is likely the better strategy. Reinvesting allows compound growth to work on a larger base. Taking dividends as cash makes more sense when you need the income, typically in retirement or when your portfolio is large enough to generate meaningful monthly amounts.

The Latte Factor is a Lie — What Actually Moves the Needle When Building Wealth

By Amora·February 22, 2026·5 min read
☕

Have you ever read a personal finance article that told you to stop buying coffee so you could become wealthy? Haha yeah. Me too. Many times. And every time I read one I felt this mix of frustration and guilt — like my R40 cappuccino was the reason I was not financially free yet.

I want to push back on this narrative. Not because I think spending money carelessly is fine — it is not. But because I think the "latte factor" advice misses the point so fundamentally that it might actually be making people's financial situations worse, not better.

What Is the Latte Factor?

The "Latte Factor" is a concept popularised by financial author David Bach. The idea is that small daily expenses — like a R40 coffee every morning — add up to significant amounts over time. R40 per day is R1,200 per month, R14,600 per year. Invested over 30 years at 10% it could grow to over R2.8 million. Bach's argument: cut the coffee, invest the savings, become wealthy.

The maths is technically correct. The problem is with what it implies about why most people are not building wealth — and what they should do about it.

Why This Advice Misses the Point

The latte factor assumes your financial problem is that you spend too much on small luxuries. But research on household finances consistently shows that most people's financial struggles come from big structural costs — housing, transport, debt repayments, and income that has not kept pace with inflation. Not coffee.

If your rent is R8,000 per month and your income is R15,000, cutting your coffee saves you R1,200 per month. That is helpful but it does not fix the structural problem. Getting a promotion, a second income stream, or moving to cheaper accommodation could save you R2,000 to R5,000 per month. That moves the needle.

What Actually Could Move the Needle

Based on my research into personal finance, the things that could make the biggest difference to someone's financial situation are: increasing income through career development, side hustles, or upskilling — this has no ceiling unlike cutting expenses which has a floor; reducing your two or three biggest expenses like housing and transport rather than dozens of tiny ones; eliminating high-interest debt which could be costing you 20% or more per year in interest; and automating savings and investments so that money is invested before you have the chance to spend it.

The difference between someone earning R15,000 and saving R1,200 per month and someone earning R20,000 and saving R2,000 per month is not coffee. It is income and intentionality about the big costs.

The Real Lesson Behind the Latte Factor

Here is what I think the latte factor is actually trying to say — and it gets lost in the coffee debate. Small, consistent habits compound over time. Spending intentionally rather than mindlessly matters. Understanding where your money goes is essential. These are valid and important points.

But the lesson should be: track all your spending and make intentional choices about what genuinely brings value to your life — not cut every small joy because someone decided your R40 coffee is what is standing between you and a million rand. Life is meant to be lived. Financial health is a long game. Both things can be true.

"You cannot cut your way to wealth. You need to earn more, spend intentionally on what matters, and invest the difference consistently. That is it."

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I track every small expense?+
Tracking all expenses for at least one to three months is genuinely useful — not to cut everything but to understand your patterns. Many people discover one or two categories where they consistently overspend without realising it. Once you know your patterns you can make intentional choices rather than rules-based restrictions.
What is the most impactful thing someone could do to improve their finances?+
Most financial researchers agree: increasing income has a higher ceiling than cutting expenses. Negotiating a salary increase, developing a high-income skill, or building a side income could transform your financial picture far more dramatically than lifestyle cuts alone. Do both — but prioritise growing the top line.

Why Would a South African Want Offshore Exposure?

The rand has lost significant value against major currencies over time. How to Talk About Money With Your Partner Without It Turning Into a Fight

By Amora·February 3, 2026·6 min read
🤝

Money is consistently cited as one of the top causes of relationship conflict. Not because couples do not love each other — but because most people never had healthy financial conversations modelled for them growing up, and because money carries so much emotional weight: security, freedom, power, self-worth, fear. When two people with different money histories and money personalities try to share a financial life, friction is almost inevitable without intentional communication.

I have been researching this topic because I think it is one of the most practically important areas of personal finance that gets overlooked. Here is what I have found about how couples could approach money conversations more effectively.

Why Money Conversations Feel So Hard

Most people never talk openly about money — not with friends, not with family, and often not even with themselves. So when a partner suddenly needs to discuss budgets or debt or savings goals it can feel incredibly vulnerable and exposing. Add to that different upbringings — one person who grew up in financial security and one who grew up in scarcity will have fundamentally different emotional responses to money conversations — and you can see why these discussions often escalate.

The goal is not to eliminate disagreement — financial values genuinely differ between people and that is okay. The goal is to create a space where both people feel safe enough to be honest.

Before the Conversation: Know Your Own Money Story

Before talking to your partner about money it helps to understand your own relationship with it. What did money mean in your household growing up? Was it scarce or abundant? Was it discussed openly or kept secret? Was spending seen as enjoyment or recklessness? Was saving seen as security or deprivation? Your money story shapes your instincts and reactions more than you might realise.

AdSense — In-Content

Practical Tips for Better Money Conversations

Choose a neutral time and place — not during or immediately after a financial stress event. A Sunday afternoon over coffee is very different from a tense conversation after discovering an unexpected expense. Set a recurring money date — a monthly check-in where you review finances together so it becomes normalised rather than a crisis event. Start with shared goals rather than individual problems — "I would love for us to save for a holiday together" opens a very different conversation than "you spend too much on clothes."

Use "I feel" language rather than "you always" or "you never" language. Financial conversations easily become accusatory. "I feel anxious when our savings drop below a certain level" is more productive than "you always spend money we do not have." Agree on a "fun money" allowance for each person — an amount each person can spend without needing to justify it to the other. This preserves individual autonomy while maintaining shared financial discipline.

Navigating Different Money Personalities

Financial therapists generally identify a few common money personalities: the saver who finds security in accumulating money, the spender who finds joy or comfort in buying things, the avoider who ignores financial matters, and the worrier who is anxious about money regardless of how much they have. Most relationships involve at least two different personalities and understanding each other's type could lead to more empathy and less judgment in financial discussions.

When to Get Professional Help

If money conflicts are persistent and damaging your relationship, a financial therapist or couples counsellor with financial expertise could be genuinely helpful. There is a growing field of financial therapy that sits at the intersection of money management and emotional health. Seeking help is not a sign of failure — it is a sign that you take your relationship and your financial future seriously enough to invest in both.

"Financial compatibility is not about having the same amount of money. It is about having enough shared values and communication skills to navigate money together."

Frequently Asked Questions

Should couples combine all their finances?+
There is no single right answer. Some couples fully combine finances, some keep everything separate, and many use a hybrid approach with shared accounts for household expenses and individual accounts for personal spending. Research suggests the hybrid model works well for many couples as it balances transparency and accountability with individual autonomy.
How do you handle financial differences when one partner earns significantly more?+
This is one of the most common sources of financial tension in relationships. Some couples split expenses proportionally to income rather than equally — each contributes a percentage of their salary to shared expenses. This can feel fairer than a 50/50 split when incomes are very different. The most important thing is that both partners agree the arrangement is fair and neither feels controlled or resentful.

What Is Net Worth and Why You Should Start Tracking Yours From Today

By Amora·January 30, 2026·6 min read
✨

When I first heard the term "net worth" I assumed it was something only wealthy people tracked. Like, only people who already had significant assets needed to know their net worth. Everyone else was just trying to make it to the end of the month.

Then I came across a different perspective: tracking your net worth from the moment you have almost nothing might be the most powerful financial habit you could build. Here is why I now believe that — and how you could start today even if your current net worth is negative.

What Is Net Worth?

Net worth is simply: everything you own minus everything you owe. Assets minus liabilities. That is it.

Assets could include: money in your bank account, investments in Easy Equities or a TFSA, a car (at current market value), any property you own, and any other valuables. Liabilities include: any outstanding debt — student loans, credit card balances, clothing account balances, personal loans, a home loan. Subtract liabilities from assets and you have your net worth. If the number is negative, do not panic — many young people start with a negative net worth because of student debt or other borrowing, and that is okay. What matters is the direction of travel over time.

Why Tracking Net Worth Matters More Than Tracking Income

Your salary is what you earn. Your net worth is what you keep and build. Someone earning R30,000 per month who spends it all has a net worth that grows slowly. Someone earning R15,000 per month who invests consistently could build a higher net worth over time. The number that actually indicates financial health and progress is not your income — it is your net worth.

Tracking it regularly — even just once a month — could change your financial behaviour in subtle but powerful ways. When you can see your net worth growing each month, even slowly, it creates motivation. When it drops it creates accountability. It makes abstract financial goals feel concrete and measurable.

How to Track Your Net Worth — Simple Method

You do not need fancy software. A simple spreadsheet works perfectly. Create a table with two columns — assets and liabilities. Update the values once a month. Add a third column for net worth (assets minus liabilities). Track it over time and watch the trend.

For assets: check your bank balance, log into Easy Equities and note your portfolio value, note any other savings. For liabilities: check all outstanding balances on any accounts or loans. It takes about 15 minutes per month once you have the spreadsheet set up.

What a Growing Net Worth Could Look Like

To be clear: I am at the beginning of this journey myself. My net worth right now is not something to brag about haha. But that is exactly why I think tracking it from now is so important — I want to see the direction of travel. A year from now, five years from now, I want to look back at this starting point and see how far the journey has come.

The people who build significant wealth over time are generally not those who earned the most. They are those who consistently built the gap between what they earned and what they owed, invested the difference, and tracked their progress along the way. That is a habit that could start at any income level and any age.

"Your net worth is not a judgement of your worth as a person. It is just a number — a starting point. And every starting point can be improved."

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good net worth for my age in South Africa?+
A commonly cited guideline is to have saved roughly one times your annual salary by age 30, three times by 40, and six times by 50. However these are very general benchmarks that assume relatively high incomes and stable employment — not the reality for many South Africans. Focus on your own trajectory rather than comparisons. Any positive movement is progress.
Should I include my car in my net worth calculation?+
You can include it at current market value but cars depreciate quickly so their contribution to net worth typically declines over time. If you have a car loan, include both the asset value and the liability. Many personal finance practitioners track net worth both with and without cars and other depreciating assets to get a clearer picture of true investable wealth.
